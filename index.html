The one they got right: Right Channel

An interruptive pop-up was appropriate — checking allergies before prescribing is a genuine safety step. The channel was right; the design was not. Everything else failed.

Kill or Keep

Three alerts. Which ones should go?

Each alert appears one at a time. Slice to kill it, or Keep it if it earns its place. There's a right answer — judge it on the numbers.

Reading the numbers: "Fires / month" = how often it pops up. "Override rate" = how often clinicians dismiss it. "Acted on" = how often they actually do something. High fires + high override = noise.

Here's what NUHS did — and what it saved.

You judged three. Here's where each one landed, and why.

⚔️

Killed · Catheter reminder (CAUTI >3 days)

Near-100% override. Infection-control experts agreed clinical education was the better channel. Turned off entirely — 263 clinician-hours saved every month, and catheter-infection rates stayed unchanged.

⚔️

Killed · Non-ED orders routed to ED nurses

This alert asked nurses to review orders they had no authority to act on. Removed from their queue — 50,910 interruptive alerts eliminated every month.

🛡️

Kept · NSAID prescribed for an asthma patient

Low firing rate, 64% acted on, a specific and actionable recommendation, right population. A real clinical risk — it earns its place.

Across the wider programme, the rate of alerts acted on rose from 8% to 55%, freeing an estimated 3,600 clinician-hours a year — with no rise in safety events.

🔍

Before we turned anything off, we needed the data — who was receiving it, the override rates, what we'd monitor afterward. The data made deletion defensible. Without it, it's just a guess.

Gene–Drug Jackpot

Your genome. Your dose.

NUHS runs live pharmacogenomic alerts that match a patient's genes to the drug being ordered — before it's signed. An alert only fires when three things line up: the gene, the patient's result for it, and the drug. Pull the lever to land on a real NUHS advisory (a different one each spin), or hover a gene in the map.

In the NUHS Clinical Implementation Pilot, pre-emptive PGx panels were ordered for 3,523 patients. Of the interruptive advisories that fired, 19.7% led to a prescription change — comparable to University of Florida (~20%). Pre-emptive PGx as routine care is feasible locally.
