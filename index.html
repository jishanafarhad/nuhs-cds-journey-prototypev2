You rebuilt it. Here's what NUHS actually did.

Six of the seven principles needed fixing. Right Channel was already right — an interruptive pop-up is the correct medium for a pre-prescribing safety check.

Before
  • Generic "allergies not verified" line — no actual detail
  • Fired on every order, even during pre-charting
  • Shown to staff who could not act on it
  • Only option was Dismiss
After redesign
  • Allergy list shown inline, with a link to add new ones
  • Fires once, at medication sign-off only
  • Shown only to doctors, dentists, pharmacists & APNs
  • One-click "Mark as Reviewed" or "No Known Drug Allergies"

Kill It or Keep It

Too many alerts. Too little attention.

Not every alert deserves to interrupt a clinician. NUHS analyses firing rates, override behaviour and clinical outcomes to identify low-value alerts that can be safely retired.

Should this alert stay — or should it go?

All four were retired — here's why.

None of these were wrong to build. They simply stopped earning their interruption — and the data is what let us prove it and act.

⚔️

Retired · GTN dose warning

The rule enforced an international 0.4 mg maximum, but locally only 0.5 mg tablets exist, so every order tripped it. The threshold was realigned to the local formulary.

~4,200false alerts / month removed
0.4 → 0.5 mgthreshold corrected
~100%were false positives
⚔️

Loosened · Duplicate therapy (artificial tears)

A near-zero-risk pairing — a daytime and a night-time eye lubricant — firing a High duplicate warning. One of 22 low-value duplicate classes reviewed and suppressed or loosened.

~13,000overrides / month removed
22classes reviewed
very lowclinical risk
⚔️

Retired · CAUTI catheter reminder

Often prompted when the chart was opened — before the clinician was with the patient — so no action could be taken, and the response was buried under truncated buttons. Infection-control experts agreed clinical education was the better channel; catheter-infection rates were monitored after removal.

9,992alerts / month eliminated
21.9 hrsclinician time / month
↔CAUTI rates unchanged
⚔️

Retired · Order placed by Non-ED specialty

Fired to the whole ED team — including nurses who could not act — with only "OK" to click. The same information already lived in the ED tracking board and chart summary.

You can't govern what you can't see.

A dashboard turns thousands of invisible firings into something you can question. Without it, every alert feels necessary and none can be challenged; with it, the noise takes shape and you can tell which alerts earn their interruption.

Sometimes the safest intervention is silence.

Turning an alert off is a clinical decision, and the data is what makes it defensible. The goal is never more alerts; it is safer care with less friction. Often the most protective thing a CDS team can do is take an alert away.

Gene–Drug Jackpot

Your genome. Your dose.

Pharmacogenomics uses a person's genes to guide medication selection and dosing for safer, more effective treatment. NUHS has implemented live pharmacogenomic CDS that matches a patient's genes, genomic result and prescribed medication before an order is signed.

Pull the lever to discover a real NUHS pharmacogenomic advisory — or explore the gene map to see how genetics changes prescribing.
In the NUHS Clinical Implementation Pilot, pre-emptive PGx panels were ordered for 3,523 patients. Of the interruptive advisories that fired, 19.7% led to a prescription change — comparable to University of Florida (~20%). Pre-emptive PGx as routine care is feasible locally.
